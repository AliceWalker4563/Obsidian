
##### Bacteria are small single-celled organisms, but they are very complex. They can survive in extreme temperatures, from above boiling to below freezing. Deinococcus radiodurans, can even survive extremely high levels of radiation. Bacteria's food sources vary widely, including sugar, starch, sunlight, sulfur, and iron. 
##### Bacteria belong to a life category called Prokaryotes. In prokaryotes, the DNA is not enclosed inside a nucleus. The only prokaryotes are bacteria and archaea. All other life forms are Eukaryotes, which have cells with nuclei. Viruses are not cells. 

##### Bacteria were some of the earliest life forms on Earth.They helped change the planet's environment and created atmospheric oxygen, allowing other life forms to develop. Scientists believe that more complex cells developed when free-living bacteria started living inside other cells, eventually becoming organelles. The mitochondria in your cells, which make energy, are an example of such an organelle.

##### Bacteria species have three basic shapes: rod-shaped (bacilli), ball-shaped (cocci), and spiral-shaped (like Borrelia). Some bacteria live as individual cells, while others cluster in pairs, chains, or other groupings.

##### Bacteria live almost everywhere on Earth: in soil, water, air, human body, Arctic ice, and volcanic vents. Your skin has about 100,000 bacteria per square centimeter, and a single teaspoon of topsoil contains billions of bacteria.

##### Some bacteria move using whip-like structures called flagella. They rotate these flagella like motors to move through liquids and can change direction. Other bacteria secrete a slime layer and glide over surfaces like slugs, while some do not move much.

##### Some bacteria are photosynthetic; they make their own food from sunlight and release oxygen, similar to plants. Other bacteria absorb food from their environment. Some can live on unusual "foods" like iron or sulfur.
