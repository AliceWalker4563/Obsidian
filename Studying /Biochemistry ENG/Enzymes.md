
> The science of enzymes is **enzymology** […õnňĆza…™ňąm…íl…ôd íi]. The term **enzyme** [ňą…õnza…™m] was introduced by J. Berzelius: ‚Äúcatalysts supplied by living cells.‚ÄĚ

### Characteristics of Enzymes:

- **Similarities with all catalysts:**
	- Not consumed in the reaction,
	- Accelerate only those reactions that are thermodynamically feasible without them,
	- Do not shift the equilibrium position of a chemical reaction; they only speed up its attainment.

- **Features unique to enzymes:**
	- By nature, **exclusively proteins** (ribozymes [ňąra…™bo äza…™mz] are not classified as enzymes),
	- Nearly 100% yield of the desired product (the enzyme accelerates only one specific reaction pathway, yielding exclusively that product),
	- Exceptionally high catalytic efficiency under mild conditions‚ÄĒno need for high temperature or pressure (e.g., at 25‚ÄĮ¬įC, urea decomposition to ammonia is accelerated by **10¬Ļ¬≥-fold** in the presence of the enzyme),
	- **Specificity** [sp…ôňĆs…™f…™ňąs…™ti] of action (selectivity‚ÄĒrecognizes specific functional groups or structural motifs in the substrate),
	- **Regulatability** [ňĆr…õ…°j äle…™t…ôňąb…™l…ôti] (e.g., via phosphorylation/dephosphorylation, calmodulin [ňĆk√¶lňąm…íd í äl…™n]-dependent kinases, allosteric [ňĆ√¶l…ôňąst…õr…™k] enzymes).

### Specificity

**Absolute specificity**: the enzyme catalyzes the transformation of only one specific molecule.
**Relative (group) specificity**: the enzyme acts on a group of structurally related substrates.  
	  *Examples*:  
- **Pepsin** [ňąp…õps…™n] cleaves peptide bonds **before** aromatic amino acids,  
- **Trypsin** [ňątr…™ps…™n] cleaves **after** lysine and arginine,  
- **Chymotrypsin** [ňĆka…™mo äňątr…™ps…™n] cleaves **after** aromatic amino acids,  
- **Carboxypeptidase A** [ňĆk…ĎňźrňĆb…íksiňąp…õpt…™ňĆde…™s e…™] removes amino acids one by one from the C-terminus; its rate depends on the amino acid type‚ÄĒit does **not** cleave proline or arginine.

Specificity is determined by the amino acid composition of the **active site** [ňą√¶kt…™v sa…™t].  
For instance:  
- In **trypsin**, the active site contains **aspartate** (negatively charged), which attracts positively charged side chains (Lys, Arg).  
- In **chymotrypsin**, the active site is large and lined with hydrophobic and uncharged polar amino acids to accommodate aromatic residues.

### Regulatability

Examples include:
- **Phosphorylation/dephosphorylation**: Glycogen phosphorylase [ňą…°la…™k…ôd í…ôn f…ísňąf…ír…™le…™s] (converts glycogen to glucose-1-phosphate) is active **only** in its phosphorylated form. A kinase phosphorylates it; a phosphatase dephosphorylates it.
- **Calmodulin-dependent kinases**: Calmodulin changes conformation upon binding Ca¬≤‚Āļ ions, thereby activating the kinase.
- **Allosteric enzymes**: Possess, in addition to the active site, an **allosteric regulatory site** where an effector binds non-covalently, inducing a conformational change that modulates enzyme activity.

### Active Site

The active site is typically located in a **structural cleft**‚ÄĒa depression in the protein globule. This cleft allows the substrate to be surrounded by numerous amino acid residues from all sides.

The location of the active site correlates with function:  
- In **endoglucanase** [ňĆ…õndo äňą…°luňźk…ôňĆne…™s] (cleaves internal glycosidic bonds in cellulose), the active site is **exposed** on the surface.  
- In **exoglucanase** [ňĆ…õkso äňą…°luňźk…ôňĆne…™s] (processively cleaves from chain ends), the active site lies within a **tunnel-like loop** through which the substrate chain threads.

The active site **does not exist** in the primary structure of the protein. It forms only during **folding**. Amino acid residues that constitute the active site may be distant in the linear sequence but become spatially adjacent after proper folding.

### Cofactors

- **Inorganic**:
  - Metal ions (e.g., Mg¬≤‚Āļ, Zn¬≤‚Āļ, Fe¬≤‚Āļ/Fe¬≥‚Āļ),
  - Iron‚Äďsulfur clusters (e.g., [2Fe‚Äď2S], [4Fe‚Äď4S]).
- **Organic**:
  - Nucleotide-derived (e.g., AMP, FAD, NAD‚Āļ),
  - Vitamin derivatives (e.g., ascorbic acid [vitamin C], nicotinamide [from vitamin B‚āÉ]),
  - Aliphatic compounds (e.g., glutathione [ňą…°luňźt…ôőła…™ňĆo än]).

**Metal-activated enzymes** can function (albeit poorly) without their metal ion, whereas **metalloenzymes** are absolutely dependent on metals (e.g., nitrogenase [ňąna…™tr…ôd í…ôňĆne…™s] requires Mo and Fe; peroxidase [p…ôňąr…íks…™ňĆde…™s] requires heme-Fe; őĪ-amylase [ňą√¶lfa…™ ňą√¶m…™ňĆle…™s] requires Ca¬≤‚Āļ).

Nicotinamide-containing coenzymes consist of a nitrogenous base (nicotinamide), ribose, and phosphate groups.  
- **NAD‚Āļ** (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide): oxidized form  
- **NADH**: reduced form  

Dehydrogenases using NAD‚Āļ are called **nicotinamide-dependent**, while those using FAD are **flavoproteins**.  
- **FAD** (flavin adenine dinucleotide) [fl√¶v…™n ňą√¶d…ôňĆniňźn da…™ňąnjuňźkli…ôňĆta…™d]: oxidized form  
- **FADH‚āā**: reduced form  

Cofactors are classified as:
- **Coenzymes** [ňąko äňĆ…õnza…™mz]: loosely bound (e.g., NAD‚Āļ),
- **Prosthetic groups** [ňąpr…ísőł…õt…™k …°ruňźps]: tightly/covalently bound (e.g., FAD in succinate dehydrogenase).

Glutathione is a tripeptide (Glu‚ÄďCys‚ÄďGly), but it is **non-ribosomal**: the ő≥-carboxyl group of glutamate forms a peptide bond‚ÄĒthis cannot be synthesized on ribosomes.

**Apoenzyme** [ňĆ√¶po äňą…õnza…™m] + **cofactor** = **holoenzyme** [ňĆho älo äňą…õnza…™m]

#### Functions of the two-component enzyme:
- **Apoenzyme**:
  - Determines substrate specificity,
  - Enhances the catalytic efficiency of the cofactor.
- **Cofactor**:
  - Directly participates in catalysis,
  - Mediates interaction between apoenzyme and substrate,
  - Stabilizes the apoenzyme structure,
  - In some enzymes, cofactors serve as **photosensors**‚ÄĒswitching the enzyme to its active state upon light absorption.

### Enzyme Classification

#### By reaction type (established in 1961 by the Enzyme Commission):
1. **Oxidoreductases** [ňĆ…íks…™do är…™ňąd Ćkt…™siz] (electron transfer),
2. **Transferases** [ňątr√¶nsf…ôre…™siz] (transfer of functional groups),
3. **Hydrolases** [ňąha…™dr…ôňĆle…™siz] (hydrolysis reactions),
4. **Lyases** [ňąla…™e…™siz] / **synthases** (cleavage or formation of C=C, C=O, etc., without ATP),
5. **Isomerases** [a…™ňąs…ím…ôre…™siz] (intramolecular rearrangements),
6. **Ligases** [ňąla…™…°e…™siz] / **synthetases** (bond formation‚ÄĒC‚ÄďC, C‚ÄďS, C‚ÄďO, C‚ÄďN‚ÄĒwith ATP hydrolysis),
7. **Translocases** [tr√¶nsňąlo äke…™siz] (transport of ions/molecules across membranes).

Each enzyme has a unique **EC number** (Enzyme Commission number) with four digits:  
**EC a.b.c.d**  
- *a* = class,  
- *b* = subclass,  
- *c* = sub-subclass,  
- *d* = serial number.

**Examples**:  
- **EC 1.18.6.1**: nitrogenase (reduces N‚āā to NH‚āÉ),  
- **EC 2.1.3.3**: ornithine carbamoyltransferase (forms citrulline from carbamoyl phosphate + ornithine),  
- **EC 3.5.3.1**: arginase (hydrolyzes arginine ‚Üí urea + ornithine),  
- **EC 4.3.2.1**: argininosuccinate lyase (cleaves argininosuccinate ‚Üí arginine + fumarate),  
- **EC 5.4.2.2**: phosphoglucomutase (converts glucose-6-phosphate ‚ÜĒ glucose-1-phosphate),  
- **EC 6.3.4.5**: argininosuccinate synthetase (joins aspartate to citrulline),  
- **EC 7.1.2.2**: ATP synthase (synthesizes ATP from ADP + PŠĶĘ).

### Isoenzymes (Multiple Molecular Forms of Enzymes ‚Äď MMFE)

**Multiple molecular forms of enzymes (MMFE)** are a group of enzymes that catalyze the **same reaction** in a single biological species but differ in structure and physicochemical properties: activity, substrate affinity, response to regulators, electrophoretic mobility. **Over half** of all enzymes exist as MMFE.

*Example*: **Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)** [ňąl√¶kte…™t ňĆdiňźha…™ňądr…íd í…™ne…™s], EC 1.1.1.27, converts pyruvate ‚ÜĒ lactate.  
It is a **tetramer** composed of two subunit types:  
- **M** (muscle-type),  
- **H** (heart-type).  

Possible combinations: M‚āĄ, H‚āĄ, M‚āÉH, M‚āāH‚āā, MH‚āÉ, etc.  
- **H-rich isoforms**: predominant in heart, high affinity for lactate, inhibited by high pyruvate.  
- **M-rich isoforms**: predominant in skeletal muscle, optimized for rapid pyruvate ‚Üí lactate conversion during anaerobic glycolysis.  
These isoforms are separable by **electrophoresis**.

#### Classification of MMFE:
- **Isoenzymes** (genetically distinct forms):
  - **Genetically independent proteins**: encoded by different genes,
  - **Allozymes** [ňą√¶l…ô äza…™mz] (products of different alleles in heterozygotes),
  - **Heteropolymers**: oligomers formed from subunits encoded by different genes (e.g., LDH).
- **Post-translational variants** (from a single gene):
  - **Metazymes** [ňąm…õt…ôza…™mz] (covalently modified forms (e.g., phosphorylated, AMPylated, glycosylated)),
  - **Homopolymers**: oligomers of identical subunits; activity may vary with oligomer size (dimer vs. tetramer),
  - **Conformers** [k…ônňąf…Ēňźrm…ôrz] (same sequence but different 3D conformations (includes all allosterically regulated enzymes)).
